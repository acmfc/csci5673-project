Bottlenecks

  
  Most traffic breakdowns on real freeways, i.e., in open systems, are caused by bottlenecks. A "bottleneck" is just a local decrease of the road capacity, for example
  • Onramps and intersections ( Onramp Scenario, )
  • lane closings (black region in the Lane Closing Scenario, ) or narrowings at road works
  • uphill or downhill gradients as in the Uphill Grade Scenario Scenario,
  • and traffic lights as an example of a time-dependent bottleneck.
One can show that the macroscopic (i.e., large-scale) properties of traffic jam propagation are essentially determined by two factors
  1. Traffic demand: Inflow and, if applicable, ramp flows,
  2. bottleneck strength indicating the severety of a bottleneck, of whatever nature, with just a single number: The reduction of the capacity (maximum throughput) compared to that of the neighboring links.
For the generation of traffic breakdowns and subsequent jams, a third "ingredient" comes into play: The internal perturbation of the traffic flow, e.g., by sudden braking or lane-changing maneuvers. This can be simulated by the button.

Simulating Speed Limits

 By simulating the Lane Closing Scenario, we can demonstrate that imposing sensible speed limits may indeed have positive effects. Speed limits are implemented by reducing the model parameter "desired velocity" to the value of the speed limit. Thus, chosing in Scenario 3 100% trucks is equivalent to imposing a speed limit at the desired velocity of the trucks (80 km/h).
  • For 100% trucks (speed limit is active) at a flow rate of 1360 vehicles/hour, all vehicles manage to change lanes before the location of the lane closing.
  • Now lift the speed limit and assume 20% trucks by changing the percentage of incoming trucks from 100 to 20. Although the static capacity (maximum equilibrium flow) of the new situation is much higher, you will observe a traffic breakdown at the lane closing!

"How can this be?"

Besides the decrease of the theoretical capacity, there is a second effect which dominates in most situations: Homogenization . Because only fast drivers are affected, speed limits cut the upper tail of the distribution of desired velocities (Here: reduce the double peak distibution to a single delta peak at 80 km/h). This leads to more homogeneous traffic with less perturbations by abrupt braking manoeuvres. Because such perturbations trigger most traffic breakdowns, the net effect of speed limits is beneficial.

"Bad news for all the speeders ammong you!"

However, for sufficiently low traffic flow, breakdowns cannot be triggered even by arbitrarily strong perturbations. The simulations suggest that in this case speed limits are not necessary.

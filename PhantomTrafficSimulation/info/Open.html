Simulation of Open Boundaries

  
Most theoretical studies of taffic dynamics pertain to closed systems, i.e. ring roads. There simplicity appeals theoretical physicists. Real freeways, however, are open systems. Remarkably, simulated traffic breakdowns in open systems lead to a different dynamics compared to that in closed systems. The main differences are
  • One has to impose nontrivial boundary conditions at the inflowing and outflowing boundary.
  • The main control parameter is the traffic flow, in contrast to the traffic density in closed systems
  • Combining open systems with bottlenecks (which always are present in reality!) leads to types of congested traffic that are not known from closed systems. For example, breakdown from free to homogeneously flowing congested traffic ( scenarios 3 and 4), "triggered stop-and go waves", or the coexistence of different congested states. (to show these, one needs larger system than can be displayed here).
The implementation of boundary conditions is tricky because information can flow in both directions
  • downstream by the motion of each single vehicle
  • upstream because stop-and-go waves and other collective motions travel against the driving direction
To obtain a mathematically well-posed problem and to avoid overdetermination, the boundary conditions have to be dynamically adapted during runtime.
Martin Treiber
